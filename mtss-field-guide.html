Tier Architecture

Three tiers, each doing its job.

When the architecture is sound, Tier 1 reaches roughly 80%, Tier 2 reaches 15%, and Tier 3 serves the few who need intensive support. When that math breaks, the system tells you exactly where to look.

I
Tier 1 · Universal
≈80%
of students

Universal supports for every student.

The instructional and behavioral foundation that every student receives. High-quality core curriculum, evidence-based instructional moves, and proactive behavior expectations — applied consistently across the school.

Academic lane
High-quality core instruction, differentiation, common formative assessment, universal screening 3×/year.
Behavior / SEL lane
Schoolwide behavior expectations, SEL curriculum, classroom routines, restorative practices baseline.
II
Tier 2 · Strategic
≈15%
of students

Targeted, time-bound support — on top of Tier 1.

Small-group instruction or behavior support layered on top of the universal experience — never as a replacement for it. Time-bound, progress-monitored, and exited as students respond.

Academic lane
Targeted small-group reading or math intervention, double-dose blocks, structured scaffolds aligned to core content.
Behavior / SEL lane
Check-In/Check-Out, small-group SEL, restorative circles, mentor matching, structured de-escalation routines.
III
Tier 3 · Intensive
≤5–10%
of students

Intensive, individualized — and progress-monitored weekly.

The smallest tier by design. Highly individualized, evidence-based, and supported by frequent progress monitoring. Tier 3 is where the most specialized expertise and the most coordinated team-based decisions live.

Academic lane
1:1 or 1:2 specialized instruction, intensive intervention with daily progress monitoring, individualized goals.
Behavior / SEL lane
FBA + BIP, wraparound team meetings, individualized counseling, family-school coordination, safety plans.
The Two Disciplines

When Tier 1 isn't reaching 80%, diagnose the system first.

MTSS isn't a referral pipeline. It's a diagnostic system. These two disciplines are what keep it honest.

The threshold

The 80% rule.

Tier 1 — your universal experience — should serve at least 80% of learners well. If fewer than 80% of students are succeeding with Tier 1 alone, you do not refer the 20% upward. You diagnose Tier 1 itself.

This is the principle that flips a deficit-based system into an architecture-based one. The data below the threshold is a signal to review fidelity — not a referral trigger.

The diagnostic

The ICEL framework.

When Tier 1 isn't reaching 80%, MTSS asks where the system is failing — before asking who needs more support. The diagnostic is Instructional Framework → Curriculum → Environment → Learner. In that order.

Without ICEL, the 80% rule is just a number. With ICEL, it becomes a non-blaming root-cause framework that protects students, supports educators, and surfaces the real lever for change.

ICEL — The Diagnostic Order

Look at the system before the learner.

The order matters. Skipping straight to the learner is what breaks an MTSS system from inside.

I
Instructional Framework
Is there a coherent, system-level instructional framework supporting practice across classrooms?
C
Curriculum
Is the content itself standards-aligned, paced realistically, and high-quality enough to do the job?
E
Environment
Are the conditions of learning — time-on-task, classroom climate, schedule structure — protecting student access?
L
Learner
Last, not first. Examined only after the prior three have been ruled out as the system-level cause.
